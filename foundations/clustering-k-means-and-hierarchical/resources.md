# Resources: Clustering (k-means & hierarchical)

All links verified 2026-07-11; the two book links below verified 2026-07-12.

- 📺 [StatQuest: K-means clustering](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4b5d3muPQmA)
  (video) — Josh Starmer walks through k-means with tiny numeric examples and clear
  animations, exactly the style of this lesson. Watch after the lesson's toy example
  to see the assign-and-update loop animated.
- 📺 [StatQuest: Hierarchical Clustering](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7xHsRkOdVwo)
  (video) — companion video covering agglomerative clustering, dendrograms, and
  linkage types. Pairs well with the lesson's hierarchical section.
- 🎮 [K-Means Clustering Simulation](https://user.ceng.metu.edu.tr/~akifakkus/courses/ceng574/k-means/)
  (interactive, free) — add points by clicking, pick k, and step through the
  algorithm iteration by iteration. Great for building intuition about how starting
  positions affect convergence and how cluster boundaries move.
- 📖 [Stats and R: Clustering analysis by hand and in R](https://statsandr.com/blog/clustering-analysis-k-means-and-hierarchical-clustering-by-hand-and-in-r/)
  (long post, free) — covers both k-means and hierarchical clustering with
  hand-worked examples and matching R code, including all three linkage methods.
  Good as a second-pass reference after doing the practice problems.

- 📚 [An Introduction to Statistical Learning](https://www.statlearning.com/)
  (book, free PDF) — the source this lesson's hierarchical clustering, linkage, and
  dendrogram material is cited to. Chapter 12 (Unsupervised Learning) covers k-means,
  hierarchical clustering, and the scaling/linkage/robustness cautions in the
  lesson's Common traps. Read after the lesson when you want the fuller statistical
  treatment.
- 📚 [Introduction to Applied Linear Algebra (VMLS), Boyd & Vandenberghe](https://web.stanford.edu/~boyd/vmls/)
  (book, free PDF) — chapter 4 is the source for this lesson's k-means objective and
  the assign-update algorithm, built from vectors and distances only (no statistics
  background needed). Good second pass on the k-means half, with striking real
  examples (clustering handwritten digits and documents).
