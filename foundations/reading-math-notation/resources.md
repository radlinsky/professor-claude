# Resources: How to read a formula out loud

All links verified 2026-07-03.

- 📖 [Basics of Mathematical Notation for Machine Learning](https://machinelearningmastery.com/basics-mathematical-notation-machine-learning/)
  (post, ~3–4k words) — written for exactly this learner: a programmer who reads code
  fluently but hits a wall at notation. Covers the same conventions this lesson does —
  the hats/bars/tildes on letters, the Greek alphabet with pronunciations, summation
  and product, set membership — with worked examples. Read it after the lesson as a
  second pass in a different voice.
- 📚 [Glossary of mathematical symbols (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_mathematical_symbols)
  (reference) — the lookup table to keep open in a tab. Sorted from beginner symbols
  outward, each entry gives the symbol's name, its LaTeX, and its context-specific
  meanings. Use it the way you'd use `?function` in R: when a formula throws a mark you
  don't recognize, search it here.

## 🔤 Going deeper on Greek vs Latin

This lesson's "Greek = unknown truth, Latin = your data" rule is a strong guess, not a
law. The [mean, variance & covariance resources](../mean-variance-covariance/resources.md)
have a full deep-dive on exactly how far that instinct carries — the hat override, the
capital-Greek-looks-like-Latin trap, and the real rule underneath (*parameter vs
statistic*). Worth reading once you've met a few Greek letters in the wild.
