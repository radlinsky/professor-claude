Clustering partitions a collection of $N$ $n$-vectors $x_1, \ldots, x_N$ into $k$ groups so that vectors in each group are close to each other, as measured by Euclidean distance. The $k$-means algorithm is the most widely used method: it alternates between assigning each vector to its nearest group representative and updating each representative to be the centroid of its group, monotonically decreasing a clustering objective until convergence. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, ch. 4]

**Also known as:** $k$-means clustering, Lloyd's algorithm (after Stuart Lloyd, who first proposed it in 1957); group representatives are also called cluster centroids

## Definition(s)

*Clustering* is the task of grouping or partitioning $N$ $n$-vectors $x_1, \ldots, x_N$ into $k$ groups (clusters), with the vectors in each group close to each other. A clustering is specified by an $N$-vector of *group assignments* $c$, where $c_i \in \{1, \ldots, k\}$ is the group to which vector $x_i$ is assigned. The set of indices in group $j$ is $G_j = \{i \mid c_i = j\}$. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, pp. 69, 72]

Each group has an associated *group representative* (an $n$-vector) $z_1, \ldots, z_k$. These need not be members of the original data set. The *clustering objective* is the mean squared distance from each vector to its assigned representative:
$$J^{\text{clust}} = \bigl(\lVert x_1 - z_{c_1} \rVert^2 + \cdots + \lVert x_N - z_{c_N} \rVert^2\bigr) / N.$$
A smaller $J^{\text{clust}}$ means a better clustering. The extreme $J^{\text{clust}} = 0$ occurs only when the data take exactly $k$ distinct values and each vector equals its representative. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, pp. 72--73]

ISL formulates $K$-means as minimizing total within-cluster variation: $\min_{C_1,\ldots,C_K} \sum_{k=1}^{K} W(C_k)$, where $W(C_k) = (1/|C_k|) \sum_{i,i' \in C_k} \sum_{j=1}^{p} (x_{ij} - x_{i'j})^2$ is the within-cluster variation defined using pairwise squared Euclidean distances. This is equivalent to Boyd's formulation because the within-cluster pairwise sum equals twice the sum of squared distances to the centroid. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 515--517, Eqs. 12.15--12.18]

**Hierarchical clustering** is an alternative to $K$-means that does not require pre-specifying the number of clusters. It produces a tree-like representation of the data called a *dendrogram*. In *bottom-up* (agglomerative) hierarchical clustering, each observation starts as its own cluster; at each step the two most similar clusters are fused, until all observations belong to one cluster. Cutting the dendrogram at a given height produces a partition into a specific number of clusters. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 519--524]

**Linkage** defines the dissimilarity between two groups of observations, extending pairwise dissimilarity to clusters. The four most common types are: *complete* (maximal pairwise dissimilarity), *single* (minimal pairwise dissimilarity), *average* (mean pairwise dissimilarity), and *centroid* (dissimilarity between centroids). Average and complete linkage are generally preferred because they yield more balanced dendrograms; single linkage can produce extended trailing clusters; centroid linkage can produce inversions. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 523--524, Table 12.3]

## Notation

| $G_j$ | "group j" | The set of indices $\{i \mid c_i = j\}$, i.e., the indices of vectors assigned to group $j$. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 72] |

| $J^{\text{clust}}$ | "J-clust" | The clustering objective: mean squared distance from data vectors to their representatives. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 72] |

## Key results & derivations

- **Optimal assignment given fixed representatives** -- When the representatives $z_1, \ldots, z_k$ are fixed, $J^{\text{clust}}$ is minimized by assigning each vector $x_i$ to the group of its nearest representative: $c_i = \arg\min_j \lVert x_i - z_j \rVert$. Ties are broken by choosing the smallest index $j$. The resulting $J^{\text{clust}}$ is the mean of the squared distances to the nearest representative. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, pp. 73, 75]
- **Optimal representative given fixed assignments** -- When the group assignments are fixed, $J^{\text{clust}}$ is minimized by choosing each representative $z_j$ to be the centroid (mean) of the vectors in its group: $z_j = (1/|G_j|) \sum_{i \in G_j} x_i$. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 74]
- **The k-means algorithm** -- (Algorithm 4.1) Given initial representatives $z_1, \ldots, z_k$, repeat until convergence: (1) *Partition*: assign each $x_i$ to the group of the nearest representative; (2) *Update*: set each $z_j$ to the mean of the vectors in group $j$. The algorithm terminates when the assignments do not change between iterations. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, pp. 74--75]
- **Monotonic decrease of objective** -- Each step of the $k$-means algorithm either decreases or leaves unchanged the clustering objective $J^{\text{clust}}$: step 1 minimizes $J^{\text{clust}}$ over assignments with representatives fixed; step 2 minimizes $J^{\text{clust}}$ over representatives with assignments fixed. Since $J^{\text{clust}}$ is bounded below by zero and decreases in each step, the algorithm converges in a finite number of steps. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 76]
- **Suboptimality** -- The $k$-means algorithm is a heuristic; it finds a "very good" clustering (low $J^{\text{clust}}$) but cannot guarantee a globally optimal solution. Different initial representatives can lead to different final partitions with different objective values. A common practice is to run $k$-means multiple times with different initializations and keep the result with the lowest $J^{\text{clust}}$. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 76]
- **Complexity** -- Each iteration of $k$-means requires approximately $(3k + 1)Nn$ flops: $3Nkn$ for the nearest-neighbor step and $Nn$ for the centroid update. The total is of order $Nkn$ per iteration. Each run typically takes fewer than a few tens of iterations. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 79]
- **Centroid minimizes mean squared distance** -- Exercise 4.1 proves that the vector $z$ minimizing $J(z) = \lVert x_1 - z \rVert^2 + \cdots + \lVert x_L - z \rVert^2$ is the centroid $\bar{x} = (x_1 + \cdots + x_L)/L$. The key step is showing that $\sum_{i=1}^{L}(x_i - \bar{x})^\top (z - \bar{x}) = 0$, so $J(z) = \sum \lVert x_i - \bar{x} \rVert^2 + L \lVert z - \bar{x} \rVert^2 > J(\bar{x})$ for any $z \neq \bar{x}$. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 87]
- **Pairwise-sum identity connects the two k-means formulations** -- $(1/|C_k|) \sum_{i,i' \in C_k} \sum_j (x_{ij} - x_{i'j})^2 = 2 \sum_{i \in C_k} \sum_j (x_{ij} - \bar{x}_{kj})^2$, where $\bar{x}_{kj}$ is the mean of feature $j$ in cluster $k$. This shows that minimizing within-cluster pairwise variation (ISL) is the same as minimizing sum of squared distances to centroids (Boyd). The centroid update step minimizes the right-hand side, and reassigning observations can only improve it. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, p. 517, Eq. 12.18]
- **K-means finds a local optimum; multiple random starts are essential** -- The K-means algorithm converges to a local optimum that depends on the initial random cluster assignments. Running it multiple times (e.g., `nstart = 20` or `50` in R) from different random starts and selecting the run with the smallest total within-cluster variation is strongly recommended. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 517--518; @boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 76]
- **Hierarchical clustering algorithm** -- (Algorithm 12.3) Start with $n$ singleton clusters. Repeat: fuse the two clusters with the smallest inter-cluster dissimilarity, recording the height of each fusion. The result is a dendrogram. The fusion height indicates how different the two fused clusters are; observations fusing near the bottom are most similar. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 523--524]
- **Dendrogram interpretation** -- Similarity of two observations is read from the *vertical axis* (the height at which their branches first fuse), NOT from horizontal proximity. Two leaves that are close together on the $x$-axis may fuse at a very high point and thus be quite dissimilar. There are $2^{n-1}$ possible leaf reorderings of a dendrogram, all equally valid. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 520--522]
- **The nesting assumption can be violated** -- Hierarchical clustering assumes clusters at finer resolution are nested within coarser ones. If the true structure is not hierarchical (e.g., gender cross-cuts nationality), hierarchical clustering can give worse results than $K$-means for a given number of clusters. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, p. 523]
- **Choice of dissimilarity measure matters** -- Euclidean distance is the default but not always appropriate. Correlation-based distance considers two observations similar if their features are highly correlated (similar profiles), even if they are far apart in Euclidean distance. The choice depends on whether magnitude or shape matters for the application. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 525--527]

## Misconceptions & learner traps

- **k-means does not find the global optimum** -- Because $k$-means is a heuristic that depends on initialization, it can converge to a local minimum. Learners sometimes assume the algorithm finds the best possible clustering. Running it multiple times with different starting points and keeping the best result mitigates this. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 76]
- **Empty groups can appear** -- During the partition step, some groups may end up with no assigned vectors. When this happens, the group (and its representative) is dropped, resulting in fewer than $k$ groups. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 75]
- **Visual clustering is misleading** -- Scatter plots make clustering look easy for 2-D data, but real applications typically have $n \gg 2$ (hundreds or thousands of features), where visual inspection is impossible. The algorithm works purely from distances. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, pp. 69--70]
- **Choosing k is not automatic** -- The $k$-means algorithm requires $k$ as input; it does not determine the number of clusters. A common approach is to run for several values of $k$ and compare the objective values, looking for an "elbow" where increasing $k$ gives diminishing returns. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 76]
- **"Horizontally close leaves in a dendrogram are similar"** -- Dendrogram similarity is read from the vertical axis (fusion height), not horizontal proximity. Two observations can be adjacent on the $x$-axis yet fuse at a great height, meaning they are no more similar to each other than to many other observations. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 521--522]
- **"Clustering results are the truth"** -- Clustering methods are not very robust to perturbations: removing a subset of observations and re-clustering can produce quite different results. Clustering results should be treated as a starting point for hypothesis generation, not as definitive findings. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 529--530]
- **"Small decisions don't matter in clustering"** -- The choice of dissimilarity measure, linkage type, whether to scale the variables, and the number of clusters all have large effects on the results. There is no single right answer; ISL recommends trying several choices and looking for patterns that consistently emerge. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 528--530]

## Teaching insights & analogies

- **Two-step iteration as a general computational idea** -- Boyd & Vandenberghe frame $k$-means as an instance of a general strategy: when jointly optimizing over two variables is hard, alternate between optimizing each one with the other fixed. Each sub-problem is easy, and the objective improves at every step. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 74]
- **MNIST digit discovery** -- Applying $k$-means with $k = 20$ to 60,000 handwritten digit images (784-dimensional vectors) produces group representatives that look like recognizable digits, even though the algorithm knows nothing about handwriting or digits. This demonstrates that $k$-means can "discover" meaningful structure from distances alone. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, pp. 79--81]
- **Document topic discovery** -- Clustering 500 Wikipedia articles (represented as 4423-dimensional word-count histograms) into $k = 9$ groups produces clusters that correspond to recognizable topics: boxing, holidays, general knowledge, music, sports, TV series, wrestling, actors, and movies. The algorithm uses only word-count distances, with no understanding of language. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, pp. 82--85]
- **Interpreting representatives** -- The entries of a group representative $z_j$ give the average feature values for the group. For voter clusters based on demographic data, $(z_3)_4 = 37.8$ means the average age of voters in group 3 is 37.8. This makes the clusters actionable. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 76]
- **Clustering for classification** -- After clustering labeled data and labeling each group by hand, new vectors can be classified by assigning them to the nearest group representative. This gives a rudimentary classifier (e.g., handwritten digit recognition). [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 85]
- **Recommendation engine** -- Clustering user listening-history vectors groups users with similar taste. For a user in cluster $j$, recommend songs with high values in $z_j$ that the user has not listened to. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 85]
- **Imputing missing entries** -- To guess missing entries of a vector, find the nearest representative using only the known entries, then fill in the missing entries from that representative. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, pp. 85--86]
- **K-means algorithm progression (Figure 12.8).** ISL illustrates K-means step by step on simulated 2D data: random initial assignment, centroid computation, reassignment, new centroids, until convergence. The visual progression makes the alternating optimization transparent. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 517--518]
- **Local optima from different starts (Figure 12.9).** ISL shows six K-means runs ($K = 3$) from different random initializations: three achieve the best objective (235.8) and three achieve worse ones (320.9, 310.9). This makes the case for multiple random starts vivid. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 518--519]
- **Dendrogram cutting (Figure 12.11).** ISL shows the same dendrogram cut at height 9 (giving 2 clusters) and height 5 (giving 3 clusters), demonstrating that one dendrogram represents a family of clusterings for all possible numbers of clusters from 1 to $n$. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 520--522]
- **Nine-observation dendrogram misreading (Figure 12.12).** ISL uses a 9-observation example to demonstrate that observations 9 and 2 appear close horizontally in the dendrogram but actually fuse at the same height as observations 8, 5, and 7 -- they are not especially similar. This concretely illustrates the horizontal-proximity misconception. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 521--522]
- **Linkage comparison (Figure 12.14).** Side-by-side dendrograms using average, complete, and single linkage on the same data make the structural differences between linkage types visible: single linkage produces long trailing clusters; average and complete produce more balanced trees. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 524--526]
- **Socks-and-computers scaling example (Figure 12.16).** An online retailer selling socks and computers: with raw counts, socks (high frequency) dominate the dissimilarity; after scaling to unit SD, both products contribute equally; after converting to dollars, computers (high price) dominate. This illustrates that scaling decisions are domain-specific and consequential. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 527--528]
- **Euclidean vs. correlation-based distance (Figure 12.15).** Three observations on 20 variables: observations 1 and 3 are close in Euclidean distance but weakly correlated; observations 1 and 2 are far in Euclidean distance but highly correlated. This shows that the two measures capture fundamentally different notions of similarity -- magnitude vs. profile shape. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 525--527]

## How the field talks about it

The name "k-means" has been used since the 1960s. The algorithm was first proposed by Stuart Lloyd in 1957 and independently by Hugo Steinhaus; it is sometimes called "Lloyd's algorithm." [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 74] The clustering found by $k$-means is called *suboptimal* because it may not achieve the globally smallest $J^{\text{clust}}$; finding the true optimum is computationally intractable for all but the smallest problems. [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 73] The term "centroid" for the group mean vector is standard; "group representative" is Boyd & Vandenberghe's more general term (since it applies even when the representative is not the centroid, e.g., before the update step). [@boyd2018appliedlinearalgebra, p. 74] "Hierarchical clustering" usually means bottom-up (agglomerative) clustering; top-down (divisive) clustering exists but is less common. "Dendrogram" is the tree diagram produced. "Complete linkage" and "average linkage" are the most commonly used linkage types. When a paper says "we used Ward's method," it is a linkage method equivalent to minimizing total within-cluster variance (closely related to $K$-means). "Mixture models" are a soft alternative to $K$-means where each observation has a probability of belonging to each cluster rather than a hard assignment. In R, `kmeans()` performs $K$-means and `hclust()` performs hierarchical clustering; `dist()` computes the pairwise distance matrix. [@james2021introductionstatisticallearning, pp. 514--530]
